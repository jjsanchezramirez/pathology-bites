Question 1


A 38 week gestational age placenta showed chronic villitis involving the majority of the villi. Representative H&E and cytomegalovirus (CMV) immunohistochemical stain images are shown above. What is the best classification for this placental abnormality?

  1. High grade chronic villitis, diffuse
  2. High grade chronic villitis, patchy
  3. Low grade chronic villitis, focal
  4. Low grade chronic villitis, multifocal
Answer 1
A. High grade chronic villitis, diffuse. Chronic villitis involving > 30% of villi is classified as high grade, diffuse villitis. Diffuse villitis or a plasmacytic component to the chronic inflammatory infiltrate (the latter is absent in this case) warrants investigation for an infectious etiology, particularly CMV. In addition, there is stem vessel obliteration and avascular villi in these photos. Answer B is incorrect because there are > 30% of villi involved. Answers C and D are incorrect because there is more than limited involvement of villi.

Reference: Chronic lymphocytic villitis
Question 2


What is the clinical association related to the placental lesion shown above?

  1. Gestational hypertension
  2. Gram positive, group B streptococcal infection
  3. Intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR)
  4. Maternal diabetes
Answer 2
C. Intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR). A subset of villitis of unknown etiology (VUE) is associated with obstetric complications ranging from IUGR and low weight for gestational age to intrauterine fetal demise. VUE excludes an infectious etiology by definition. Answers A and D are incorrect because there is no direct relationship or association of VUE with maternal diabetes or gestational hypertension. Answer B is incorrect because group B streptococcal infection is associated with acute chorioamnionitis.

Question 3


A 32 year old with no prenatal care presents with premature rupture of membranes and delivers an infant at approximately 31 weeks with Apgars of 5 and 6 at 1 and 5 minutes. At delivery, a maternal fever of 101.5 °F and foul smelling amniotic fluid are identified. The placental surface and fetal membranes are green tinged and opaque. The umbilical cord reveals the features seen in the submitted image (PAS stain). Which of the following gross features would most likely be found on careful macroscopic examination of the umbilical cord?

  1. Excessive twisting
  2. Excessively long length
  3. Peripheral white plaque(s)
  4. True knot(s)
  5. Umbilical vein thrombosis
Answer 3
C. Peripheral white plaque(s). While often no macroscopic lesions are seen in cases of mild-moderate umbilical vasculitis or funisitis, gross findings are evident in some circumstances. A common gross finding with funisitis is the presence of peripheral white, creamy or yellowish plaques / lesions, which are often due to infection by Candida species (which is present in the above image). Additionally, white concentric rings may be appreciated around umbilical vessels. This finding is usually associated with necrotizing funisitis and represents the accumulation of dying cells, cellular debris and associated immune complexes that aggregate as arcs around vessels (analogous to the precipitin arcs seen in an Ouchterlony immunodiffusion assay).

Reference: Umbilical vasculitis and funisitis
Question 4
A 19 year old G1P0 delivers a healthy term infant with post due dates (40.5 weeks) and Apgars of 9 and 9 at 1 and 5 minutes. Meconium fluid is identified at delivery. Which of the following is the most likely histologic finding in the umbilical cord?

  1. Arteritis involving one or both umbilical arteries
  2. Concentric calcifications around umbilical vessels
  3. Necrotizing funisitis
  4. Phlebitis involving the umbilical vein
  5. 3 vessel vasculitis (panvasculitis)
Answer 4
D. Phlebitis involving the umbilical vein. Mild phlebitis (inflammation specifically of the umbilical vein) is a relatively common finding with meconium in the amniotic fluid. Presumably, this is due to the early / developing fetal response toward an injurious agent (meconium).

Question 11


Which of the following is the pTNM stage (per the AJCC 8th edition) of the gestational trophoblastic neoplasm in the image above? The tumor involves the uterus and bilateral ovaries with metastases to the lung.

  1. pT1 M0, FIGO stage I
  2. pT1 M1a, FIGO stage III
  3. pT2 M0, FIGO stage II
  4. pT2 M1a, FIGO stage III
  5. pT2 M1b, FIGO stage IV
Answer 11
D. pT2 M1a, FIGO stage III. The figure shows a trophoblastic tumor with geographic necrosis, consistent with an epithelioid trophoblastic tumor. Any tumor with M1a, regardless of the pT status, is staged as FIGO stage III. Answers A and B are incorrect because metastasis to other genital structures is present, which makes this case a pT2 tumor. Answer C is incorrect because this case shows metastasis to the lung, classifying it as a M1a tumor. Answer E is incorrect because the tumor shown would be classified as stage III not IV as it does not extend to other sites outside of the reproductive organs or lungs.

Reference: Staging-gestational trophoblastic neoplasms
Question 12
A 32 year old woman was diagnosed with a placental site trophoblastic tumor. She had a missed abortion 2 years prior to diagnosis. Her serum hCG before surgery was 550 mIU/mL. Imaging did not identify any metastatic foci. The patient underwent a total hysterectomy and bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy. The tumor was found to measure 6.5 cm in greatest dimension with focal extension into the right ovary. What is her FIGO stage and prognostic score?

  1. FIGO stage I; risk score 4
  2. FIGO stage I; risk score 5
  3. FIGO stage II; risk score 4
  4. FIGO stage II; risk score 7
  5. FIGO stage III; risk score 7
Answer 12
D. FIGO stage II; risk score 7. Involvement of the ovary and the lack of metastasis makes this a pT2 M0 tumor, which corresponds to FIGO stage II. The patient is < 40 years old (score 0) with an antecedent abortion (score 1) which occurred 2 years prior (score 4). Her pretreatment hCG is below 1,000 IU/mL (score 0) and her largest tumor size is 6.5 cm (score 2). She has no metastatic disease or involvement beyond the reproductive organs (score 0 + 0) and no prior treatment (score 0). Taken together, her prognostic score is 0 + 1 + 4 + 0 + 2 + 0 + 0 + 0 = 7.

Reference: Staging-gestational trophoblastic neoplasms

Question 24
A neonate and placenta are delivered vaginally at 40 weeks 4 days gestation. APGAR scores are 9/9. The placenta shows diffuse green staining of the membranes but is otherwise unremarkable. Microscopic examination of the fetal membranes would show which of the following changes?

  1. Cysts that contain secretions and are lined by extravillous trophoblasts within the chorion
  2. Diffuse infiltration of neutrophils in the amnion and chorion, including presence of microabscesses
  3. Macrophages demonstrating yellow-brown granular pigment, which stains positive on Prussian blue
  4. Vacuolization of the amniotic epithelium, heaping up of amniotic cells and edema of the amnion
Answer 24
D. Vacuolization of the amniotic epithelium, heaping up of amniotic cells and edema of the amnion. Green staining of the fetal membranes generally refers to meconium staining and the amnion epithelial changes listed are characteristic degenerative changes associated with meconium staining. Answer B is incorrect because it depicts severe acute chorioamnionitis. Answer C is incorrect because the yellow-brown granular pigments that stain positive for Prussian blue are indicative of hemosiderin pigment rather than meconium. Answer A is incorrect because it describes a pseudocyst (or chorionic cyst).

Comment Here

Reference: Meconium staining

Question 46
 


The entity shown above was identified in the placenta of a female infant delivered via normal vaginal delivery at 38 weeks gestation to a 35 year old primipara. Which of the following pathologies is most likely to also be present in the placenta?

  1. Acute villitis
  2. Chorangiosis
  3. Chronic villitis
  4. Delayed villous maturation
Answer 46

Question 47
How is eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis diagnosed?

  1. Histological examination of the placenta
  2. Macroscopic placental examination
  3. Maternal complete blood count (CBC)
  4. Third trimester ultrasound scan
Answer 47
A. Histological examination of the placenta. At present, there are no recognized laboratory, radiological or macroscopic features that indicate the presence of eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis. Diagnosis can only be made at present by histological identification of a mixed T cell, eosinophilic and histiocytic infiltrate within the vessel wall of ≥ 1 chorionic, umbilical or stem villous vessel in the absence associated neutrophil polymorphs. Answer B is incorrect because macroscopic examination involves visually inspecting the placenta for gross abnormalities, such as size, shape and visible lesions or infarctions. However, eosinophilic / T cell vasculitis requires microscopic evaluation to identify specific cellular infiltrates and detailed vascular changes that cannot be seen with the naked eye. Answer C is incorrect because while a CBC can provide information about the overall health of the mother, including white blood cell counts and the presence of eosinophilia (an increased number of eosinophils), it does not provide direct information about the cellular composition or specific inflammatory processes within the placenta. Diagnosing eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis requires examination of placental tissue. Answer D is incorrect because ultrasound scans are used to assess the placenta and fetus's physical and functional status, such as placental location, blood flow and fetal growth. While they can identify some placental abnormalities, they cannot detect specific cellular infiltrates or the detailed histopathological changes characteristic of eosinophilic / T cell vasculitis. Ultrasound is not capable of providing the microscopic detail necessary for this diagnosis.

Question 67


Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis of this well circumscribed placental mass near the chorionic plate?

  1. Chorangioma
  2. Inflammatory myofibroblastic tumor
  3. Intervillous thrombus
  4. Leiomyoma
  5. Placental infarct
Answer 67
A. Chorangioma. Chorangiomas are likely hamartomas of the placental stem villi forming vascular masses nearly always peripheral and adjacent to the chorionic plate. They are well demarcated, firm masses when grossly identifiable and most are incidental and microscopic. Leiomyomas from the uterus can be adherent to the basal plate of the placenta and are extremely rare masses, extraneous to the placental parenchyma. Placental infarcts are typically firm and gritty triangular or rectangular masses at the maternal floor (not the chorionic plate). Intervillous thrombi are laminated irregularly shaped red or white soft masses anywhere in the placenta. Placental associated inflammatory myofibroblastic tumors are also unusual extrinsic masses that can be attached to the placenta basal plate or membranes.

Question 68
What are complications associated with chorangioma?

  1. Fetal and maternal hydrops
  2. Malignant transformation
  3. Maternal visceral hemangiomas
  4. Obstructed labor and delivery
Answer 68
A. Fetal and maternal hydrops. Vascular steal and heart failure due to fetal perfusion of a large chorangioma can lead to fetal hydrops. Maternal mirror syndrome is a rare but serious complication of hydrops fetalis. Malignant transformation does not occur in chorangiomas; if malignancy occurs, the diagnosis is likely an intraplacental choriocarcinoma, which can have associated hypervascular villi. Visceral hemangiomas in the mother are not associated with chorangiomas but fetal / neonatal hemangiomas of the skin have been reported. Chorangiomas rarely reach a size which could complicate delivery.
